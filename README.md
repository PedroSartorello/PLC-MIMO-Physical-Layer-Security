In this undergraduate thesis, physical layer security (PLS) is investigated in power line communication (PLC) systems employing multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) techniques in low-voltage residential networks, considering the frequency range from 1.8 to 100 MHz. To this end, a $2 \times 2$ MIMO wiretap channel model is adopted, in which transmission is performed in Delta differential mode and reception in Star mode.
In this context, Alice (legitimate transmitter), Bob (legitimate receiver), and Eve (passive eavesdropper) share the same physical medium, under the premise that Alice does not have access to Eve's channel state information (CSI). Accordingly, the analysis consists of comparing five distinct configurations (SISO-SE, MISO-SE, MISO-ME, MIMO-SE, and MIMO-ME), which differ in the number of transmit and receive ports used by Alice, Bob, and Eve, with the objective of evaluating the secrecy gain provided by applying MIMO techniques to PLC systems.
Two PLS metrics are employed for this purpose: the secrecy outage probability (SOP) and the effective secrecy throughput (EST), together with their associated wiretap code rates. The simulations consider two scenarios: one in which Alice knows Bob's CSI and another in which this information is unavailable. The numerical results show that the MIMO-SE configuration achieves the best performance among the evaluated alternatives, providing the lowest SOP and the highest EST in both scenarios. Therefore, the use of MIMO technology in PLC systems not only increases channel capacity but also significantly enhances PLS against passive eavesdroppers.
